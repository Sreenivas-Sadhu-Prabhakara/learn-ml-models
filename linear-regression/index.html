The whole idea, in one picture

Machine learning questions come in two flavours. Lesson 01's question was “which bucket does this deal belong to?” Today's question is “how much?” — not “is this parcel heavy or light?” but “what does it weigh?”.

CLASSIFICATION (Lesson 01)

The answer is a category: BULK or BLOCK, spam or not spam, cat or dog. You judge it with accuracy — how often was it right?

REGRESSION (today)

The answer is a number: ₹ crore, kilograms, marks, tomorrow's temperature. “Right” and “wrong” become “close” and “far off”.

Linear regression's entire trick: draw the best straight line through the examples you've seen, then read new answers off the line. Like a tailor who has measured hundreds of customers and can glance at your height and estimate your kurta length — no tape needed.

Fit the line once. For any new deal, go up from its quantity, hit the line, read across.

How do you judge a number-guessing machine?

Accuracy makes no sense here — a guess of ₹99.9 crore for a ₹100 crore deal is “wrong”, but brilliantly wrong. Regression uses two report cards, and you'll compute both for every model today:

MAE — MEAN ABSOLUTE ERROR

“On average, how many crore off?” Take each guess, measure how far it landed from the truth, average those misses. Smaller is better, and it's in real units — ₹ crore — so your gut can judge it.

R² — “R-SQUARED”

“What share of the variation does the model explain?” 0 means it knows nothing beyond the average; 1 means it explains everything. It can even go below 0 — worse than just guessing the average every time.

The golden habit of this lesson: before judging any model, first measure the dumbest possible strategy — always predict the average. Every model must beat that, or it has learned nothing. We'll compute this baseline explicitly in Step 5 and compare everything against it.

STEP 4 OF 12

Split the data: 75% training, 25% testing

The honesty rule from Lesson 01 applies to regression unchanged: the model studies the training 75% and sits its final exam on the test 25% it has never seen.

Two things from Lesson 01 are missing here, deliberately: no balancing (that was a classification fix — every deal has a value, so there's nothing to balance) and no stratify (that keeps a category mix even — a number has no categories).

STEP 5 OF 12

The dumbest possible model: always predict the average

Before any real model, we measure the strategy that requires zero intelligence: whatever the deal, guess the average. This is the score every model must beat.

CELL 5 · PYTHON
# Step 5: The dumbest possible model — always predict the average
average_value = y_train.mean()
print(f"The average training deal: ₹{average_value:.2f} crore")

always_average = [average_value] * len(y_test)

print(f"Baseline MAE: {mean_absolute_error(y_test, always_average):.2f} crore")
print(f"Baseline R²:  {r2_score(y_test, always_average):.3f}")
WHAT YOU SHOULD SEE
The average training deal: ₹31.29 crore
Baseline MAE: 42.17 crore
Baseline R²:  -0.000

Two curiosities, both healthy: the average miss (₹42.17 crore) is bigger than the average deal itself — that's the monster deals from Step 3 blowing up the misses. And R² shows −0.000 instead of a clean 0.000 only because the training average is a hair different from the test average. By definition, always-guessing-the-average explains nothing.

STEP 6 OF 12

Practical example 1: one clue — quantity alone

Now the real thing. We give linear regression exactly one clue — how many shares changed hands — and let it find the best straight line from quantity to value. Training actually computes the line (unlike KNN, which just memorised).

CELL 6 · PYTHON
# Step 6: One clue — can quantity alone predict a deal's value?
model_one_clue = LinearRegression()
model_one_clue.fit(train_df[["quantity"]], y_train)

predictions = model_one_clue.predict(test_df[["quantity"]])
print(f"MAE: {mean_absolute_error(y_test, predictions):.2f} crore")
print(f"R²:  {r2_score(y_test, predictions):.3f}")
WHAT YOU SHOULD SEE
MAE: 38.42 crore
R²:  0.110

Honest scorecard: misses shrank from ₹42.17 to ₹38.42 crore, and the model explains about 11% of the variation. Better than dumb — genuinely — but modest. Knowing how many shares traded without knowing at what price only gets you so far.

That's what an 11% R² looks like.

STEP 8 OF 12

Practical example 2: two clues — quantity and price

Surely adding the price per share fixes it? With two clues the model fits the best tilted plane instead of a line — same idea, one more dimension.

CELL 8 · PYTHON
# Step 8: Two clues — quantity AND price together
two_clues = ["quantity", "price_inr"]

model_two_clues = LinearRegression()
model_two_clues.fit(train_df[two_clues], y_train)

predictions = model_two_clues.predict(test_df[two_clues])
print(f"MAE: {mean_absolute_error(y_test, predictions):.2f} crore")
print(f"R²:  {r2_score(y_test, predictions):.3f}")
WHAT YOU SHOULD SEE
MAE: 36.40 crore
R²:  0.122

Barely moved: ₹38.42 → ₹36.40 crore, R² 0.110 → 0.122. Both clues are in the model's hands and it still can't crack the problem. Why?

A straight-line model can only weigh and add. Its guess is always (weight₁ × quantity) + (weight₂ × price) + a constant. But a deal's true value is quantity multiplied by price — 10 lakh shares at ₹2 is ₹2 crore, while 10 lakh shares at ₹2,000 is ₹2,000 crore. Same quantity, wildly different values. No amount of weighing-and-adding can express “multiply these two clues with each other”. The model isn't stupid; the shape we allowed it is too simple.

STEP 9 OF 12

Feature engineering: build the perfect clue ourselves

If the model can't multiply, we can. We create a new column — deal_size = quantity × price ÷ 1,00,00,000 — the deal's value in crore, computed by us. Inventing new clues out of old ones is called feature engineering, and it's where real ML projects are won.

CELL 9 · PYTHON
# Step 9: Feature engineering — build the perfect clue ourselves
# 1 crore = 1,00,00,000 rupees, so: value in crore = quantity × price ÷ 1e7
train_df["deal_size"] = train_df["quantity"] * train_df["price_inr"] / 1e7
test_df["deal_size"]  = test_df["quantity"]  * test_df["price_inr"]  / 1e7

three_clues = ["quantity", "price_inr", "deal_size"]
model_engineered = LinearRegression()
model_engineered.fit(train_df[three_clues], y_train)

predictions = model_engineered.predict(test_df[three_clues])
print(f"MAE: {mean_absolute_error(y_test, predictions):.4f} crore")
print(f"R²:  {r2_score(y_test, predictions):.6f}")

my_deal = pd.DataFrame({"quantity": [2_000_000], "price_inr": [950.0]})
my_deal["deal_size"] = my_deal["quantity"] * my_deal["price_inr"] / 1e7
print(f"Invented deal (20 lakh shares at ₹950): predicted ₹{model_engineered.predict(my_deal)[0]:.2f} crore")
WHAT YOU SHOULD SEE
MAE: 0.0000 crore
R²:  1.000000
Invented deal (20 lakh shares at ₹950): predicted ₹190.00 crore

From “off by ₹36 crore” to “off by less than ₹1 lakh”, with the same model. The only thing that changed was the clue.

Honest teaching moment: a perfect score is normally a RED FLAG. In a real project, R² = 1.0 almost always means the answer leaked into the clues — someone accidentally fed the model the very thing it was supposed to predict. That's precisely what we did here, on purpose: in this dataset trade_value_crore literally is quantity × price ÷ 1e7, so our “clue” is the answer wearing a moustache.

The real lesson survives the trick: crafting the right clue matters more than choosing a fancy model. In real projects you don't know the exact formula — but ratios, logs and differences of existing columns often get you almost-as-good clues. When you see a suspiciously perfect score, don't celebrate. Investigate.

STEP 10 OF 12

Set an overfitting trap

Overfitting is the student who memorises last year's question papers — perfect marks in practice, disaster in the real exam. Models do it too: given many clues and few examples, a model starts “explaining” random coincidences. Let's deliberately create those conditions:

  • Everyday deals only (₹1–100 crore) — otherwise a few ₹1,000-crore monsters drown out everything we want to show.
  • Hide the price. Step 9 proved that with quantity and price the game is over — the model just rebuilds the formula. Real projects rarely hand you the full formula, so: quantity only.
  • Only 200 training deals. With 89,367 examples our models were too well-fed to misbehave. Scarcity invites trouble.
  • Six clues: quantity wearing four costumes (raw, square root, log, log²) — heavily overlapping — plus two columns of pure random noise that mean nothing at all.
CELL 10 · PYTHON
# Step 10: Set an overfitting trap — few examples, messy overlapping clues
# Everyday deals only (₹1–100 crore), and this time the model may NOT see the price.
everyday_train = train_df[train_df["trade_value_crore"].between(1, 100)]
everyday_test  = test_df[test_df["trade_value_crore"].between(1, 100)]

tiny_train = everyday_train.sample(n=200, random_state=42)   # only 200 deals to learn from

rng = np.random.default_rng(42)

def make_clues(df, rng):
    clues = pd.DataFrame(index=df.index)
    clues["quantity"]        = df["quantity"]              # the real clue...
    clues["sqrt_quantity"]   = np.sqrt(df["quantity"])     # ...and the same clue again,
    clues["log_quantity"]    = np.log10(df["quantity"])    #    wearing two more costumes
    clues["log_quantity_sq"] = np.log10(df["quantity"]) ** 2
    clues["noise_1"]         = rng.normal(size=len(df))    # pure nonsense
    clues["noise_2"]         = rng.normal(size=len(df))    # more pure nonsense
    return clues

X_train_wide = make_clues(tiny_train, rng)
X_test_wide  = make_clues(everyday_test, rng)
y_train_wide = tiny_train["trade_value_crore"]
y_test_wide  = everyday_test["trade_value_crore"]

scaler = StandardScaler()
X_train_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X_train_wide)
X_test_scaled  = scaler.transform(X_test_wide)

print("Training deals:", len(X_train_wide), "| Test deals:", len(X_test_wide))
print("Clues:", list(X_train_wide.columns))
WHAT YOU SHOULD SEE
Training deals: 200 | Test deals: 20084
Clues: ['quantity', 'sqrt_quantity', 'log_quantity', 'log_quantity_sq', 'noise_1', 'noise_2']

We scaled the clues (Lesson 01's Step 7 trick) because Ridge and Lasso fine big weights — that only works fairly if all clues speak at the same volume. The exam is still honest: 20,084 unseen test deals.

STEP 11 OF 12

Ridge and Lasso: penalties that keep weights humble

Plain regression has one goal: fit the training deals as tightly as possible — even if that means wild weights that “explain” noise. Ridge and Lasso add a fine for big weights, so a weight has to earn its keep:

  • Ridge (L2): fines the square of each weight. Shrinks everything towards zero, deletes nothing — an editor who tones down every sentence.
  • Lasso (L1): fines the size of each weight. Happily pushes useless weights to exactly zero — an editor who strikes whole sentences out.

This fining trick has a formal name — regularisation — and Ridge and Lasso are its two most famous flavours. The dial alpha sets how harsh the fine is. We settled on alpha=10 for Ridge and alpha=3 for Lasso after a little trial and error.

CELL 11 · PYTHON
# Step 11: Plain regression vs Ridge vs Lasso — same clues, same 200 deals
plain = LinearRegression().fit(X_train_scaled, y_train_wide)
ridge = Ridge(alpha=10).fit(X_train_scaled, y_train_wide)
lasso = Lasso(alpha=3, max_iter=10000, random_state=42).fit(X_train_scaled, y_train_wide)

band_average = [y_train_wide.mean()] * len(y_test_wide)
print(f"{'Baseline':8s}  MAE: {mean_absolute_error(y_test_wide, band_average):5.2f} crore   R²: {r2_score(y_test_wide, band_average):6.3f}")

for name, model in [("Plain", plain), ("Ridge", ridge), ("Lasso", lasso)]:
    p = model.predict(X_test_scaled)
    print(f"{name:8s}  MAE: {mean_absolute_error(y_test_wide, p):5.2f} crore   R²: {r2_score(y_test_wide, p):6.3f}")
WHAT YOU SHOULD SEE
Baseline  MAE: 15.59 crore   R²: -0.002
Plain     MAE: 14.43 crore   R²:  0.022
Ridge     MAE: 14.31 crore   R²:  0.080
Lasso     MAE: 14.15 crore   R²:  0.126

Read the scoreboard: plain regression, drowning in junk clues with only 200 examples, barely improves on always-guessing-the-average (R² 0.022). Ridge's humility fine rescues it to 0.080, and Lasso — which can throw junk clues away entirely — does best at 0.126. Nobody's a genius here (it's a hard game: mid-range deals, no price), but the penalties clearly stopped the madness.

The Lesson 01 confession applies here too: we compared three models and chose the alpha dials using test-set scores, to keep the lesson short. In a real project you'd tune all of that on a validation slice of the training data, and open the test set exactly once, at the very end.

STEP 12 OF 12

Look inside: the weights tell the whole story

A linear model is completely transparent — it's just one weight per clue. Let's put the three models' weights side by side.

ModelWhat the weights reveal
PlainGone wild. A tug-of-war between overlapping clues (−115.85 on one costume of quantity, +151.99 on another, on deals worth ₹1–100 crore!) — and it even bet a couple of crore on the pure-noise columns. It “explained” coincidences in its 200 deals; the exam exposed it.

How to judge a regression model — the cheat sheet

The regression version of Lesson 01's four questions:

QuestionToolWhat to look for
1. Better than dumb luck?Always-predict-the-average baselineCompute its MAE and R² first, every single time (our Step 5). A model that can't beat it has learned nothing.
2. How far off, in money?MAEIt's in the units you care about — “off by ₹38 crore on average” is something your gut can judge. Smaller is better.
3. How much does it explain?R²0 = nothing beyond the average, 1 = everything, below 0 = worse than the average. Judge it against the baseline and the problem's difficulty, not against perfection.
4. Too perfect?SuspicionR² ≈ 1.0 usually means the answer leaked into the clues (our Step 9, done deliberately). Don't celebrate a perfect score — investigate it.
5. Was the exam fair?Train/test splitAll scores must come from the 25% the model never saw. Ours did.

Rough R² guide for messy real-world data: ~0 = learned nothing · 0.1–0.4 = found a real pattern (our honest models today lived here) · 0.5–0.9 = strong · ≈1.0 = be suspicious — the answer probably leaked into the clues.

The recipe you just learned

  1. Load the data and look at the number you're predicting — averages lie when data is skewed (Steps 2–3)
  2. Split 75% train / 25% test before anything clever (Step 4)
  3. Measure the dumb baseline first — always-predict-the-average (Step 5)
  4. Start simple — one clue, one straight line, and a picture of it (Steps 6–7)
  5. Add clues — but remember a linear model can only weigh-and-add, never multiply (Step 8)
  6. Engineer features — the right derived clue beats a fancier model; a perfect score means a leak (Step 9)
  7. Fear overfitting when clues are many and examples are few — and reach for Ridge (shrinks) or Lasso (deletes) (Steps 10–12)

Swap LinearRegression for any other regression model and steps 1–6 of the recipe don't change. Same skeleton as Lesson 01, different report cards.
