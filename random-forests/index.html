The whole idea, in one picture

In 1906, at a country fair, a scientist named Francis Galton watched about 800 visitors pay a small fee to guess the weight of a prize ox. Farmers guessed, butchers guessed, and plenty of people who had never touched cattle guessed too. Almost every single guess was wrong — some wildly so.

Then Galton averaged all the guesses. The crowd's average landed within half a kilo of the true weight — closer than almost every individual, experts included. Individually wobbly, collectively sharp. That is the entire trick behind a random forest: don't trust one opinion; average many independent ones, and their mistakes cancel out.

Most guesses miss. Their average — the green line — sits almost exactly on the truth.

One caution from the fair: the magic only works if the guesses are independent. If everyone just copies the loudest uncle in the crowd, the "average" is really one opinion wearing 800 hats. Remember that — it explains everything about how a random forest is built.

So what exactly is a random forest?

Same exam as Lessons 01 and 04: look at three numbers about a deal — quantity, price per share, total value in crore — and guess whether it was a BULK deal or a BLOCK deal. Only the model changes.

ONE TREE (LESSON 04)

Studies all the training deals and grows question after question until it can recite them. Sharp memory, jumpy judgement — shuffle the training data slightly and the whole tree can rearrange itself.

A RANDOM FOREST

200 trees, each deliberately made a little different. To classify a new deal, every tree gives its verdict and the majority vote wins — the village fair, in code.

How do you get 200 honestly different opinions out of one training set? The forest plays two tricks:

  • Different study material. Each tree trains on its own random re-sample of the training deals — drawn with repeats, so every tree misses roughly a third of the deals and sees some others two or three times.
  • Different blind spots. At every question, a tree is only allowed to consider a random subset of the clues — it can't always reach for its favourite.

Two hundred trees, each with slightly different schooling and slightly different blind spots, make different mistakes. Different mistakes cancel in a vote. Copied mistakes don't — which is exactly why the randomness is not a bug, it's the whole point.

STEP 3 OF 8

Prep the data — the Lesson 01 drill, in one cell

Identical preparation to Lesson 01, so every score today is directly comparable: keep all 8,938 BLOCK deals plus an equal random sample of BULK deals (so a coin flip scores 50%), choose the three clues and the answer, then lock away 25% as the final exam.

CELL 3 · PYTHON
# Step 3: Prep the data — same drill as Lesson 01, condensed into one cell
# (a) Balance: keep all BLOCK deals + an equal random sample of BULK deals
block_deals = deals[deals["deal_type"] == "BLOCK"]
bulk_deals  = deals[deals["deal_type"] == "BULK"].sample(n=len(block_deals), random_state=42)
data = pd.concat([block_deals, bulk_deals])

# (b) Clues (X) and answer (y)
features = ["quantity", "price_inr", "trade_value_crore"]
X = data[features]
y = data["deal_type"]

# (c) Split: 75% for learning, 25% hidden away for the exam
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(
    X, y,
    train_size=0.75,
    random_state=42,
    stratify=y,
)

print("Balanced deals:", len(data), "(half BULK, half BLOCK)")
print("Deals for training:", len(X_train))
print("Deals for testing: ", len(X_test))
WHAT YOU SHOULD SEE

Deals for training: 13407

Where did the scaling step go? Lesson 01's KNN measured distances, so giant quantity numbers bullied the small ones and we needed StandardScaler. Trees don't measure distance — they ask yes/no threshold questions like "is the share price above ₹319.30?" — in fact, that is the exact first question the full-depth tree you'll grow in Step 4 asks on this data. Rescale a column and the tree simply moves its threshold. So trees and forests don't need scaling at all. One prep chore, gone.

First, today's baseline — a single decision tree exactly like Lesson 04's, left to grow to full depth with no restrictions. Full depth means it keeps asking questions until it can recite the training deals perfectly.

STEP 5 OF 8

Grow the forest

n_estimators=200 means 200 trees, each trained on its own random re-sample of the 13,407 training deals with its own random blind spots. This cell takes a few seconds — you are literally growing two hundred trees.

CELL 5 · PYTHON
# Step 5: Grow a forest — 200 slightly-different trees, all voting
forest = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=200, random_state=42)
forest.fit(X_train, y_train)

forest_predictions = forest.predict(X_test)
forest_accuracy = accuracy_score(y_test, forest_predictions)

print(f"One tree alone:      {tree_accuracy:.1%}")
print(f"Forest of 200 trees: {forest_accuracy:.1%}")

STEP 6 OF 8

How many voters do you actually need?

n_estimators is today's dial, like K was in Lesson 01. Let's grow forests of 1, 5, 20, 50, 100 and 200 trees and watch the score.

CELL 6 · PYTHON
# Step 6: How many trees are enough?
tree_counts = [1, 5, 20, 50, 100, 200]
scores = []

for n in tree_counts:
    model = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=n, random_state=42)
    model.fit(X_train, y_train)
    score = accuracy_score(y_test, model.predict(X_test))
    scores.append(score)
    print(f"{n:>3} trees  →  accuracy {score:.1%}")

plt.plot(tree_counts, scores, marker="o")
plt.xlabel("Number of trees in the forest")
plt.ylabel("Accuracy on the test set")
plt.title("More voters help — up to a point")
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.show()
WHAT YOU SHOULD SEE

 20 trees  →  accuracy 83.5%
 50 trees  →  accuracy 83.5%

…plus a line chart of the same numbers: a steep climb, then a flat shelf. Puzzled that "1 tree" scores 79.4% when Step 4's lone tree scored 83.0%? A forest tree never sees the full training set — it studies its own random re-sample. Alone, that handicap hurts. In a crowd, it's exactly what makes the voters different.

The pattern to remember: going from 1 to 20 voters buys about four percentage points; going from 100 to 200 buys nothing you can see. Once the crowd is big enough, its average has settled — extra voters just repeat the consensus. More trees never really hurt accuracy; they just cost more compute for nothing.

One honest confession: to keep this lesson short, we compared tree counts using the test set. In a real project that's cheating — you'd pick n_estimators using a separate slice of the training data (called a validation set), and touch the test set exactly once, at the very end.

STEP 7 OF 8

The headline act: which clue does the forest trust most?

Here is the forest's best party trick. Every time any question in any of the 200 trees cleanly separates BULK from BLOCK, the clue it asked about earns credit. Add up the credit across all 200 trees, scale it to total 100%, and you get feature_importances_ — the model telling you which clues it actually leaned on.

CELL 7 · PYTHON
# Step 7: Which clue does the forest lean on most?
importances = pd.Series(forest.feature_importances_, index=features).sort_values()

for clue, weight in importances.items():
    print(f"{clue:<18} {weight:.1%}")

importances.plot.barh(color="#0E7C52")
plt.xlabel("Share of the forest's decisions")
plt.title("Which clue matters most?")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
WHAT YOU SHOULD SEE
quantity           22.6%
trade_value_crore  36.7%
price_inr          40.7%

…plus a horizontal bar chart of the same three numbers (they sum to 100%).

Two things worth staring at:

  • Price per share is the forest's favourite single clue (40.7%) — block deals in this data tend to happen in higher-priced shares, and the forest noticed on its own.
  • No clue dominates. Remember Lesson 01's homework? Dropping trade_value_crore barely hurt the score, because value = quantity × price — the clues overlap. This chart is the same fact from the other side: when clues carry overlapping information, the forest spreads its trust across them (40.7 / 36.7 / 22.6) instead of piling onto one.

Read it right: importance says which clue was useful to this forest, not what causes a block deal. It's a window into the model's mind, not a law of the market.

How to judge any model — the cheat sheet

The same four questions as always, with today's answers filled in:

QuestionToolWhat to look for
1. Better than dumb luck?Compare with a baseline83.8% vs a 50% coin flip — and vs the stronger baselines it had to beat: KNN's 79.8% and the single tree's 83.0%.
2. Where does it fail?Confusion matrixBig numbers on the diagonal (1,989 and 1,756 here). Check which mistake it makes more — ours mislabels BULK as BLOCK more often than the reverse.
3. Can I trust a "yes"? Does it miss?Precision & recallPrecision = trustworthiness of its claims. Recall = how much it misses. Pick the one your problem cares about.
4. Was the exam fair?Train/test splitScore measured on 4,469 deals the forest never saw. Same locked-away 25% as every lesson.

Rough accuracy guide for a balanced 50/50 problem like ours: ~50% = learned nothing · 60–75% = learned something · 75–90% = solid · 95%+ = be suspicious — usually the answer leaked into the clues.

The recipe you just learned

  1. Load the data and prep it the standard way — balance, choose X and y, split 75/25 (Steps 2–3)
  2. Skip scaling — trees ask threshold questions, so units don't matter (Step 3)
  3. Train the rival first — one tree, 83.0% (Step 4)
  4. Grow the forest — 200 diverse trees voting, 83.8% (Step 5)
  5. Turn the dial honestly — accuracy climbs, then flattens; more voters stop helping (Step 6)
  6. Read the model's mind — feature_importances_ shows which clues it leaned on (Step 7)
  7. Judge properly — baseline, confusion matrix, precision & recall (Step 8)

The deeper idea travels far beyond forests: many diverse, independent opinions beat one clever opinion — in models, in markets, and at village fairs. Ensembles like this win real-world ML competitions constantly, and a random forest is the first one every practitioner learns.
